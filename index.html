State of the science — updated June 15, 2026

Time travel is real physics. The hard part is going back.

To the future, we already go — every fast-moving particle and orbiting astronaut is a proof. To the past, Einstein's equations leave a door ajar, but nature may have bolted it shut.

What's new in time-travel physics

June 11, 2026 THEORY

Retrocausal capacity of a quantum channel

Physicists published "Retrocausal Capacity of a Quantum Channel: Communicating Through Noisy Closed Timelike Curves" in Physical Review Letters. They calculated the exact information-theoretic limits on how much classical and quantum information could be sent backward through a post-selected closed timelike curve, even with noise.

What it does NOT mean: this is a mathematical bound on a hypothetical channel — not a built device that sends messages to the past.

Source: Physical Review Letters, DOI 10.1103/znyd-npk5
May 29, 2026 PROVEN THEORY

New time-crystal phase aids quantum stability

A newly characterized time-crystal-related phase of matter was reported (ScienceDaily). It points toward more robust quantum technologies by exploiting the breaking of time-translation symmetry.

What it does NOT mean: time crystals break time-translation symmetry; they are not time machines.

Source: ScienceDaily
May 4, 2026 PROVEN

Time crystal linked to a real device

Researchers connected a time crystal to a real device, a step toward using time crystals in quantum memory and sensing.

What it does NOT mean: relevant to quantum tech, not to time travel.

Source: ScienceDaily
February 2026 CONTESTED

Warp-drive claims reassessed

New analyses — "General formalism, classification, and demystification of current warp-drive spacetimes" (arXiv 2602.16495) and reassessments by Barzegar, Buchert & Vigneron, plus An T. Le's "warpax" verification toolkit — re-examined recent "physical warp drive" claims. They found that small but real energy-condition violations persist.

What it does NOT mean: the 2024 "no negative energy needed" claims are being walked back; a fully physical warp drive remains unproven.

Source: arXiv 2602.16495

2024–2025 milestones still shaping the field

University of Toronto (Daniela Angulo, Aephraim Steinberg group) measured "negative time" for photons passing through excited atoms. Interpretational group-delay effect in quantum optics, not literal past travel.

Frame of reference

Two directions of time

Future

SETTLED SCIENCE

Moving clocks tick slower; clocks in weaker gravity tick faster. Travel fast or sit near a strong gravitational source, return home, and you will have aged less than everyone else. This is not a thought experiment. It is tested daily by GPS, particle accelerators, and atomic clocks.

Past

OPEN QUESTION

General relativity permits worldlines that loop back to their own past — closed timelike curves. But every route needs ingredients humanity cannot make: exotic matter, infinitely long cylinders, cosmic strings, or warp bubbles with energy-condition violations. Quantum mechanics may forbid them entirely.

The block universe

In Einstein's relativity, "now" is not universal. Events that are simultaneous for one observer happen at different times for another. The universe can be described as a four-dimensional "block" where past, present, and future events exist in the geometry of spacetime. Your worldline is the path you trace through it.

Destination: tomorrow

Travel to the future PROVEN

You are time traveling right now — just very slowly. Every moving object and every height above the ground is a tiny time machine. The effect is called time dilation, and it is one of the most precisely verified predictions in physics.

Velocity time dilation

PROVEN

Albert Einstein, Special Relativity, 1905

The faster you move through space, the slower you move through time as measured by a stationary observer. The effect becomes dramatic only near the speed of light.

$$\tau = t \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$$

The traveler returns t − τ into everyone else's future.

Gravitational time dilation

PROVEN

Albert Einstein, General Relativity, 1915

Clocks run slower deeper in a gravitational field. Near a black hole's event horizon, time almost stops as seen from far away.

$$\tau = t \sqrt{1 - \frac{2GM}{rc^2}}$$

At the Schwarzschild radius r = 2GM/c², this factor goes to zero.

Evidence in the real world

+38 µs

GPS satellites

GPS clocks must be corrected by roughly +38 microseconds per day: about +45 µs from weaker gravity, offset by −7 µs from orbital velocity. Without the correction, navigation would drift ~10 km per day. PROVEN

1971

Hafele–Keating experiment

Atomic clocks flown around the world on commercial jets disagreed with ground clocks by tens to hundreds of nanoseconds, exactly as Einstein predicted. PROVEN

2.2 µs

Atmospheric muons

Muons created in the upper atmosphere have a lifetime of about 2.2 microseconds. They reach the ground only because time dilation extends their life in Earth's frame. PROVEN

1 mm

JILA optical-lattice clocks

In 2022, Jun Ye's group at JILA/NIST measured time ticking at measurably different rates across just one millimeter of height; later work pushed below the millimeter scale. PROVEN

0.02 s

Sergei Krikalev, cosmonaut

After 803 days in orbit, Krikalev is the human who has traveled farthest into the future — by about 0.02 seconds. PROVEN

The real future time machine

Build a ship that can reach a significant fraction of the speed of light, or park near a neutron star or black hole, then return to Earth. You will have aged less than the people you left behind. The only obstacle is engineering — fuel, shielding, acceleration — not the laws of physics. At 0.995c, a round trip to the galactic center could feel like years while millennia pass on Earth.

Destination: yesterday

Travel to the past THEORY NOT BUILDABLE

General relativity permits closed timelike curves (CTCs) — worldlines that loop back to their own past. But the keyword is "permits." Allowed by the equations is not the same as allowed by nature. Every known route requires something we cannot build or that may be physically forbidden.

Closed timelike curves (CTCs)

THEORY

General relativity

The umbrella idea: a path through spacetime that returns to its own past event. CTCs are the mathematical signature of backward time travel in curved spacetime.

Gödel's rotating universe

THEORY

Kurt Gödel, 1949

A universe rotating as a whole can drag spacetime into loops, allowing CTCs.

Catch: Our universe does not rotate that way.

Tipler cylinder

SPECULATIVE

Frank Tipler, 1974; based on van Stockum dust

An infinitely long, ultra-dense cylinder spun near light speed drags spacetime into loops.

Catch: "Infinitely long" is fatal. No real object can satisfy the requirement.

Kerr (rotating) black hole

SPECULATIVE

Roy Kerr, 1963

The interior of a rotating black hole contains a ring singularity, and the mathematics of the Kerr metric contains CTCs.

Catch: You would likely be destroyed by tidal forces or plasma, and inner-horizon instabilities probably forbid passage.

Traversable wormholes

THEORY

Morris & Thorne, 1988 (prompted by Carl Sagan for Contact)

Turn a wormhole into a time machine by moving one mouth at near-light speed, or parking it in strong gravity, so the two mouths' clocks desynchronize. Step through the right way and you exit before you entered.

Catches: Needs exotic matter with negative energy density to stay open. And you can never arrive earlier than the moment the time machine was first switched on.

Cosmic strings

SPECULATIVE

J. Richard Gott, 1991

Two infinitely long, ultra-massive cosmic strings flying past each other can create CTCs in the spacetime around them.

Catch: Cosmic strings are hypothetical and have never been detected.

Warp drives → time machines

THEORY CONTESTED

Alcubierre, 1994; Shoshany & Snodgrass, 2024

Faster-than-light warp bubbles can be combined to loop into the past. Shoshany & Snodgrass gave the first explicit model gluing two warp drives into a CTC.

Catch: Warp drives require exotic energy. As of 2026, claims that a fully physical warp drive needs no negative energy are being reassessed and walked back.

The required ingredient: exotic matter

PROVEN EFFECT NOT AT USABLE SCALE

Casimir effect, Hendrik Casimir, 1948; measured experimentally

The only lab-demonstrated negative-energy effect is the Casimir effect: two uncharged conducting plates held very close in vacuum experience a force because the region between them has lower electromagnetic energy than empty space.

Catch: Real but minuscule. Sustaining the macroscopic amounts a wormhole or warp drive needs is far beyond anything known.

Self-consistency

Paradoxes & how physics handles them

The famous grandfather paradox asks: if you travel back and prevent your own birth, what happens? Physics has proposed several answers. None has been tested. All are serious.

Novikov self-consistency principle

THEORY

Igor Novikov, 1980s

Only self-consistent histories are permitted. You can visit the past, but you cannot change it. Any action you take was always part of history. The laws of physics conspire to prevent paradoxes.

Deutsch's quantum CTCs (D-CTCs)

THEORY

David Deutsch, 1991

A self-consistency condition on quantum states removes paradoxes. The model has strange side effects, including the ability to solve certain computationally hard problems efficiently.

Post-selected CTCs (P-CTCs)

THEORY SIMULATED IN LAB

Seth Lloyd et al., 2011

A teleportation-based model selects only self-consistent outcomes. It has been simulated with photons in the lab, but only in statistics — nothing actual traveled to the past. The model stays paradox-free while preserving freedom of choice.

Tobar–Costa paradox-free model

THEORY

Germain Tobar & Fabio Costa, 2020

A mathematical proof that closed timelike curves can be made self-consistent: events in the past adjust to prevent paradoxes, allowing free will while still avoiding logical contradictions.

The June 2026 Physical Review Letters retrocausal-capacity result fits here too: it quantifies how much information such hypothetical loops could even carry, even under noise.

Counterargument

Why it might be impossible — forever

The most honest position in time-travel physics is not optimism or pessimism, but open uncertainty. Here are the strongest reasons backward time travel may never be possible.

Chronology protection conjecture

THEORY

Stephen Hawking, 1992

Hawking proposed that whenever you try to build a CTC, quantum vacuum fluctuations pile up without limit and destroy the device. The universe, he wrote, is "safe for historians." The conjecture is widely discussed but not proven.

The energy problem

THEORY

Exotic matter with negative energy density has never been produced at the macroscopic scale needed to hold open a wormhole or sustain a warp bubble. The quantum Casimir effect is real but far too weak.

The detection problem

THEORY

If backward time travel were easy, where are the time travelers? In 2009, Stephen Hawking hosted a party for time travelers, sending invitations after the event. Nobody came. It is a joke with a serious point: either travel is impossible, or it is so constrained that it leaves no obvious trace.

Reference

Glossary

Time dilation

The difference in elapsed time measured by two clocks, due to relative velocity or gravitational potential.

Lorentz factor

γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²). It quantifies how much time, length, and mass change at high speed.

Proper time

The time experienced by a clock moving along a particular worldline; the invariant "personal" time.

Spacetime / worldline

Spacetime is the four-dimensional fabric of events. A worldline is the path an object traces through it.

Lightcone

The boundary of all paths light could take from an event. The cone separates past, future, and elsewhere.

Closed timelike curve

A worldline that loops back to its own past. General relativity permits them in some spacetimes.

Geodesic

The straightest possible path in curved spacetime; the path a freely falling object follows.

Event horizon

A boundary beyond which nothing — not even light — can escape to the outside universe.

Schwarzschild radius

r = 2GM/c². The radius at which a mass becomes a black hole.

Exotic matter

Hypothetical matter that violates standard energy conditions; thought to be needed for wormholes and warp drives.

Negative energy

Energy density less than that of empty space. The Casimir effect is a small, real example.

Casimir effect

An attractive force between two uncharged conducting plates in vacuum, caused by a negative energy density between them.

Wormhole

A shortcut connecting two distant regions of spacetime. Traversable ones likely require exotic matter.

Cosmic string

A hypothetical one-dimensional defect from the early universe, immensely dense and under tension.

Chronology protection

Stephen Hawking's conjecture that quantum effects prevent the formation of closed timelike curves.

Novikov self-consistency

The principle that only self-consistent histories can occur, preventing paradoxes.

Block universe

The view of spacetime as a four-dimensional block where all events exist, and "now" is observer-dependent.

Retrocausality

The idea that an effect can precede its cause, sometimes discussed in quantum foundations.

Time crystal

A phase of matter that repeats in time without energy input, breaking time-translation symmetry.

Redshift / blueshift

Redshift: light stretched to longer wavelengths (receding). Blueshift: light compressed to shorter wavelengths (approaching). We use them here as visual metaphors for past and future.

Questions

FAQ

Can we travel to the future?

Yes — and we already do, slightly. Time dilation from speed and gravity is experimentally proven. Astronauts, GPS satellites, and atmospheric muons all experience it. The only limit to reaching the far future is engineering, not physics.

Can we travel to the past?

Not with any known, buildable method. General relativity's equations allow closed timelike curves, but every route requires exotic matter, infinite structures, or warp bubbles that may violate energy conditions. Quantum physics may forbid them entirely.

Could a paradox happen?

Leading theoretical models say no. Novikov's self-consistency principle, Deutsch's quantum CTCs, and the Tobar–Costa paradox-free model all argue that physical laws select only self-consistent histories. None of this has been tested with real time travel.

Did scientists just send a message to the past (2026)?

No. The June 2026 Physical Review Letters paper calculated the theoretical information limits of a hypothetical closed timelike curve. It is math about a possible channel, not a built device.

Is "negative time" real time travel?

No. The 2024 University of Toronto result is an interpretational effect in quantum optics, related to group delay and weak measurements. Photons did not literally travel to the past.

Why don't we see time travelers?

Possibly because backward travel is impossible, or because you cannot go back before the first machine was built. Stephen Hawking's 2009 party for time travelers — invitations sent after the event — had no guests. It is a playful but pointed argument.
